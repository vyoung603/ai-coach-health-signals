Physical Health Signals in AI Coach Conversations

March 5, 2026 Author: Val Young Prepared for: Mark

Methodology

  • Dataset: 6,893 unique users, 124,121 human-AI message pairs, 19,646 conversations. Date range: Sep 30, 2025 -- Feb 27, 2026 (~6 months).
  • Classification method: Contextual keyword matching with exclusion filters. Each category uses specific medical/behavioral terms with false-positive exclusions (e.g., "drinking" excludes "drinking water/coffee/tea"; "high" excludes "high anxiety/stress"). Not simple keyword search.
  • False positive rates: Estimated at 0-5% per category via 20-sample manual review. Categories using specific medical terminology (cancer, diabetes, opioids) have near-zero FP rates. Substance categories (alcohol ~5%, cannabis ~5%) have the highest.
  • Survivorship bias note: Users with more sessions produce more text, creating more keyword matches. Correlations between physical health mentions and session count may partly reflect this. This report does not claim causal relationships.
  • Scope: This is exploratory analysis of what users are discussing. It does not evaluate bot performance or prescribe product changes.

Open questions before any of these could be implemented

Clinical appropriateness: should an AI coach surface benefits during emotional conversations? Privacy: what consent is needed to route signals to care managers? Accuracy: would real-time classification be reliable enough to act on? These are product, clinical, and legal questions -- not data questions.

08 Open Questions

QuestionWhy It Matters
Is the correlation between physical health mentions and session count causal, or survivorship bias?If it's bias, these users aren't stickier -- they just have more text. First-session mention analysis would clarify.
Are the comorbidity lift values stable?Small sample sizes (8-38 co-occurrences for some pairs) mean lift values could shift with new data.
What does the bot do well that we're not measuring?Empathic validation may be exactly right for some of these conversations. Not every mention needs a referral.
What do users actually want when they mention physical health?We're inferring from text. User interviews would reveal whether they want resources, referrals, or just to be heard.
How would proactive benefit surfacing affect trust?Users may feel surveilled if the coach starts recommending programs based on what they disclose.
